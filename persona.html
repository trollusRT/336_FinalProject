Core constraint: Mina has low vision and chronic fatigue, which makes reading labels, managing timers, and standing for long periods difficult—especially after work.

Short Story

Mina likes the idea of cooking, but energy is unpredictable. On good days, they do simple meals. On low-energy days, they rely on freezer staples, meal kits, or easy-prep snacks. They’re not looking for “perfect” routines—just repeatable ones.

Needs & Pain Points

  • Low vision: tiny recipe text, low-contrast UIs, and cluttered layouts slow everything down.
  • Fatigue: standing 20–30 minutes can be too much; cleanup is the hidden cost.
  • Safety: forgetting burners/timers is a real anxiety point when exhausted.
  • Decision overload: too many options (recipes, groceries) can stall action.

Goals

  • Keep meals “good enough” 4–5 days a week without burning out.
  • Reduce kitchen friction with prep shortcuts and reliable defaults.
  • Use tools that support vision and memory (labels, reminders, audio).

Assistive Tools & Coping Strategies

Mina’s setup focuses on clarity, automation, and less standing.

  • Phone screen reader + zoom for recipes and grocery apps.
  • Smart speaker timers (voice-controlled, multiple timers).
  • High-contrast labels on pantry items + “big print” marker.
  • Stool in the kitchen to sit during prep.
  • One-pan / one-pot defaults to reduce cleanup.

Blog

Entries are short on purpose: quick wins, what worked, and what didn’t.

Post 1 — The “Two-Timer” Trick

February 22, 2026 • 2 min read

Today’s win: I used two timers—one for cooking, one for “check-in.” The check-in timer is the secret. It interrupts autopilot before I wander off and forget I’m cooking.

What helped: voice timers (“set a 7 minute check-in”) + keeping the stove area uncluttered.

Post 2 — Cooking When Standing Is Too Much

February 16, 2026 • 3 min read

If fatigue is high, I don’t “push through.” I switch to a sitting meal: toast + eggs, microwave rice + tuna, or a soup that doesn’t need babysitting. The goal is energy tomorrow, not heroics today.

Rule: if prep takes more than 10 minutes, it’s a “future Mina” meal.

Post 3 — Labels That Actually Work

February 03, 2026 • 2 min read

I replaced tiny printed stickers with thick-marker labels and a consistent layout: protein left shelf, carbs middle, sauces right. It’s boring—and that’s the point. Less searching = less fatigue.

Small upgrade: put “OPEN FIRST” snacks at eye-level so low-energy days don’t become “skip eating” days.

Accessibility Notes (Built Into This Page)

  • Semantic structure: header, nav, main, section, article for screen readers.
  • Skip link: jump straight to content for keyboard users.
  • Focus states: visible outlines for keyboard navigation.
  • Readable layout: generous spacing, short paragraphs, no tiny text.
  • Reduced motion support: respects system “reduce motion.”
  • User controls: high-contrast + larger text toggles (below).

Note: “Perfect contrast” depends on display settings; the high-contrast toggle is included as a practical aid.
